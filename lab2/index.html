Does Affordability Equal Livability?

Living in one of the most expensive cities in the world.

Use this interactive map and chart to compare NYC community districts across multiple livability dimensions. You can switch indicators, search for a district, and hover to see linked details in both views. This helps you quickly spot patterns, outliers, and trade-offs, and test whether lower-rent neighborhoods are actually more livable when commute time, food access, and park access are considered together.

What This View Shows

Research aim: identify relatively livable NYC community districts by comparing economic, mobility, and amenity conditions in one coordinated interface.

Working hypothesis: neighborhoods with the lowest rents are not automatically the most livable places, because commute burden, food access, and park access can substantially change total living burden.

Indicator meaning in this interface:

  • Rent-income ratio: higher values indicate stronger housing cost pressure.
  • Median rent and median income: show nominal housing and income conditions.
  • Average commute time: higher values indicate stronger mobility burden.
  • Food density: lower values may indicate weaker neighborhood service access.
  • Park area ratio: lower values may indicate less green-space access.

Interaction design: switch attributes to reexpress both map and chart, and use hover/search to retrieve linked information for the same district across views.

Data Source and Processing (with Year)

This workflow follows the source table and processing diagram used in the TLBI project. Data were compiled from mixed-year releases (primarily 2023-2025) and harmonized to NYC Community District (CD) units.

Source Inventory

  • Median Rent: CCCNY, Median Asking Rent Monthly, CSV, polygon model at CD level.
  • Median Income: CCCNY, Median Household Incomes, CSV, polygon model at CD level.
  • Commuting Convenience / Mobility Burden: U.S. DOT BTS GeoPlatform, Average Travel Time to Work, GeoJSON, polygon model at Census Tract level.
  • Amenities (Parks): NYC Open Data, Parks Properties, GeoJSON, polygon model.
  • Food Access: data.ny.gov, Retail Food Stores Map, GeoJSON, point model.
  • Boundaries: NYC Department of City Planning, Community District Boundaries, Shapefile, polygon model.

Processing Workflow

Variables were derived by joining all datasets to Community District boundaries and then computing standardized indicators:

  1. Economic Burden (E): join median rent and median income (with household filter year 2023 / all-household specification), then compute E = annual rent / income.
  2. Park Access (A): intersect park polygons with CD boundaries, dissolve by CD, sum park area, compute CD area, then calculate A = park area / CD area.
  3. Food Access (F): spatially join retail food points to CD polygons, count stores by CD, compute CD area, then calculate F = count / CD area.
  4. Mobility Burden (C): spatially join census-tract commute data to CDs, then compute population-weighted mean commute time.

Final mapped indicators in this interface are median rent, median income, rent-income ratio, food density, park area ratio, and average commute time.
